Modeling spatial wholeness in cities using information entropy theory (Peer Review)

H. Ekinoglu, A.S. Kubat, R. Plunz, A|Z ITU Journal of the Faculty of Architecture, November, 2017.

Summary

Descartes states “Divide each difficulty into as many parts as possible and necessary to resolve it.” Deconstruction is not a new idea as demonstrated by Descartes quote but a compelling one for tackling and understanding the issues behind big and complex systems especially as vast as cities. Built form is a relational process and the overall spatial form emerges with a sense of wholeness in its topologic embodiment. Built environments expose a unique hierarchy of size, scale, and shapes among the built entities that embody a certain degree of completeness. Using Alexander’s “levels of scale” property of wholeness as a morphologic translation interface, the method developed in this research allows questioning relational scaling formations among the built entities. Shannon’s Information entropy theory has been employed for measuring the state of uncertainty, and disorderliness conveyed through the multivariate context of morpho-information across varying scales. This study aims to cross-evaluate mean Entropy-IQR values generated for ten cities using proposed method with the survey results that ten experts, architects, urban planners, and landscape architects have rated for ten cities urban layouts in three aspects of the wholeness. Experts do not have an agreement about the degrees wholeness of case study cities among each other, inter raters reliability (Kα) is 0,14 and the correlation coefficient between normalized median expert views and mean H-IQR values is 25%. The results indicate that definition and sense of wholeness even for place making experts is not as intuitive as Alexander claims but quite loose. These findings help to point out the need for data-driven and evidence-informed analytical methods that measure the relative degree of wholeness in constantly changing cities. Such as the method developed in this study.
