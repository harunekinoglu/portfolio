Antakya's Historical District Renewal Area| Urban Design

This project received Honorable Mention Award in UNESCO International Urban Design & Brief Competition, World Urban Forum III

team: harun ekinoğlu - gökçe gerekli yılmaz- sevgi yücesan - müge durusu - seda patır

Summary

Antakya’s Historical District; Antioch Being counted among Rome, Alexandria, and Constantinople as one of the great metropolises of the Roman Empire; the city where the name “Christian” was first pronounced, Antakya is a city of 2300 years. The city has been under the reigns of different civilizations since its establishment, and all of these civilizations left their marks on the spatial arrangement as well as the social arena. These different periods acquire the city different, but cooperating layers and the spell of being at the human scale physically, at the generations scale symbolically and in the collective and individual perception. It is the spaces of Antakya that hides the wisdom that she strained throughout the centuries, but these spaces disappear day by day. There is illegal housing that destroys the intimacy of the historic texture.

Antakya is now the southernmost city of Turkey. Its landscape is dramatic with the river Orontes passing through the mountain and the fertile land extending from the plain. Its history goes back to the 300 B.C., when it was founded with the name of “Antioch” during the Hellenistic period, with a grid plan of streets intersecting at right angles. The urban design proposal aspires to re-create the former Bridge Gate as a meeting place -- the new Forum Antioch on the Orontes, a forum square as the city’s main center, with complementary functions in the new and the old district squares that create order and balance.

Conceptual Design Idea: “The metaphor of atom as activation energy for a model of historical districts”

The orbits in the macro scale of the city also act as the energy admitters around the nucleus. The memories of the city that emerge on the same orbital in the new and the historic districts also spatially reinforce the dialectic between the city and the citizen and create the synergy between these two areas. Thus, the historic heart stays lively and escapes from being a slum. The corridors that the orbitals obtain the city via creating the circulation patterns act as veins carrying blood between forums, thus nurturing the historical district.





The Open UNESCO / UIA / CARLETON UNIVERSITY International Student Competition has been initiated at the UNESCO’s Round Table of Experts meeting held during the WUF II in Barcelona, September 13, 2004 on the theme of “Social Sustainability of Historical Districts.” Carleton University in Ottawa, Canada has finalized IN COOPERATION WITH UNESCO AND UIA the organization of the competition on “Historical District Renewal Area.” The top twelve entries were displayed at the WUF III in Vancouver in June 2006. An analysis of all significant entries were published by the end of 2006.
Criteria for judging of the submissions included the following, as spelled out in the competition call for submissions:

  • adequacy between the urban concept and the brief’s content with regards to enhancement of social cohesion,
  • spatial integration in upgrading of an historical district with an interdisciplinary and democratic process,
  • creative blending of local materials with contemporary construction techniques in the design solution,
  • successful response of the urban design to its surrounding historical context, and
  • successful integration of novel architectural ideas or reuse of existing architecture for concepts pertaining to socio-cultural diversity and sustainability in historical districts.

Jury later on also considered the criteria of UNESCO VIENNA MEMORANDUM ON CONTEMPORARY ARCHITECTURE AND MANAGING URBAN HISTORIC LANDSCAPES SIGNED IN MAY 2005 in evaluation of the entries:

  •    ethic standards in high-quality design,
  •      urban design and art expression in their specific historical, social and economic components and transmit them to forthcoming generations,
  •     mitigation of the negative effects of traffic circulation and parking in the design of public space,
  •     renewal in planning and design modeled on the historic roof-scape and the original building plots,
  •     proportion and design fitting into the historic pattern and architecture, and
  •     contemporary architecture complementing the values of the historic urban landscape.
